What is a cultural asset?

A cultural asset is something that contributes to the community's creativity, knowledge, traditions, and meaning. A cultural asset can be a physical thing, like a building or piece of art. It can also be something intangible and temporary, like an event, ritual, or ceremony. The City of Chelsea CultureMap currently uses the following cateogries to group and organize cultural assets, recognizing that other categories exist, and that some cultural assets might fit into more than one category.

Architecture

Historic buildings, significant restorations, contemporary buildings, notable design details

Landmarks

Heritage sites, landmarks, monuments, cemeteries

Public Art

Murals, street art, sculptures, art installations

Creative Industries

Creative studios, practice spaces, creative businesses, coworking facilities, art supplies, artist live/work space

Cultural facilities

Performance spaces, exhibitions spaces, places of worship, social clubs

Food

Restaurants, bars, commercial kitchens, urban agriculture

Events + Programming

Schools, libraries, educational programming, festivals, meeting halls, other notable events

Parks + Open Space

Parks, playgrounds, natural spaces, scenic areas, community gardens

And more

These categories serve as a guide, but the community will identify other types of cultural assets!

Why map cultural assets?

Mapping cultural assets serves a number of purposes, including celebration, protection, and support of the City’s culture. The map celebrates the City’s culture by painting a colorful testimony to the City’s wide range of cultural assets. It protects the City’s culture, because placing assets on a map is an important first step towards considering how future development might impact the City’s cultural heritage. The map supports the City’s cultural assets by providing a platform for identifying existing and potential connections between cultural assets and a range of policy issues, including transportation and housing.

Placing the map online allows anyone with access to a computer, tablet, or smartphone to identify a cultural asset and to communicate why it’s important. Community members often recognize, value, and use cultural assets differently from one another. By maintaining an open process, the cultural asset map aims to reflect the community’s full range of perspectives. If you’d like to submit a cultural asset, please take the short survey. It will only take a few moments!
